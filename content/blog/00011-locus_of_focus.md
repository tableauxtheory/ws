As of late, I've found myself thinking less and less about visual novel development and more and more about my social/dating life. There seemingly is always such a balance to be had with such matters. There are seasons, and so on, and so on.

But this just isn't natural for me. My natural proclivity is to focus 100% on one thing and then, once it is finished, focus 100% on the next.

For a large portion of my years, this proclivity worked great. I was so shy, so it was easy to hide away to myself and focus on whatever I was passionate about. If I tried to be social or "have a life", I'd end up with negative feedback, so it wasn't hard to avoid such things.

But now it is different. It has been for years, but I'm still working how to fully invest in my craft while still fully caring for the people around me, as well as continue to progress towards my goals of marriage and family.

I assume, by the way, that this difficulty of balance will only be exasperated in the presence of children.

I am currently not nailing this balance. In fact, I am in fact over-focusing on the social elements of my life. I've done this once before, when I lived in Asheville. Rather than learning how to balance, however, that era was cut short due to misc. reasons.

So now I still have the task of determining how to handle this.

For context, however, it must be noticed that I still have no dependents. No serious relationship, no children, no elderly parents. Solutions may be different in such scenarios.

What I have found is that, for now, the primary element that I am missing is that my shower thoughts (or walk thoughts, etc.) tend to drift toward social. And social not in a manner that is progressing me any closer to my two big goals\*, but instead simply wanting to help the people in my life generally build more fruitful lives. This, while important, should not be my priority.

But I suppose that begs the question: how do I divert these thoughts? 

Some of it, certainly is discipline, but I don't quite think this is all.

Going back to how I mentioned that the negative feedback loops prevented me from being social in my earlier years, I think we can get a greater perspective as to why I may be struggling right now.

I just released *Everything I Know, and Everything I'm Ever Gonna Know*, a project that, while not perfect, I still consider to be very good. The feedback has been shockingly postive for such a strange and experimental little project, so I can't help but be grateful for that. However, despite this, monetarily it has been almost entirely a flop thus far. 

To work on something for 50-60 hours a week for about a year-and-a-half and need to start looking for ways to earn money after it is finished is a bit debilitating. Not unexpected, as this is a difficult industry to navigate and all of this takes time, but since we are considering the human element of feedback loops, it is a pretty difficult feedback hit to trudge through.
